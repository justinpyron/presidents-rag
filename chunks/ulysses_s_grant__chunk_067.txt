Other banks and brokerages that owned railroad stocks and bonds were ruined.[158][159] Grant, who knew little about finance, traveled to New York to consult leading businessmen on how to resolve the crisis, which became known as the Panic of 1873.[160] Grant believed that, as with the collapse of the Gold Ring in 1869, the panic was merely an economic fluctuation.[161] He instructed the Treasury to buy $10 million in government bonds, which curbed the panic, but the Long Depression, swept the nation.[160] Eighty-nine of the nation's 364 railroads went bankrupt.[162]
In 1874, hoping inflation would stimulate the economy, Congress passed the Ferry Bill.[163] Many farmers and workingmen favored the bill, which would have added $64 million in greenbacks to circulation, but some Eastern bankers opposed it because it would have weakened the dollar.[164] Belknap, Williams, and Delano told Grant a veto would hurt Republicans in the November elections. Grant believed the bill would destroy the credit of the nation and vetoed it despite their objections. Grant's veto placed him in the Republican conservative faction and began the party's commitment to a gold-backed dollar.[165] Grant later pressured Congress for a bill to strengthen the dollar by gradually reducing the greenbacks in circulation.
