Vetoes
As Congress and its Republican-led Senate sent Cleveland legislation he opposed, he often resorted to using his veto power.[10] He vetoed hundreds of private pension bills for American Civil War veterans, believing that if their pensions requests had already been rejected by the Pension Bureau, Congress should not attempt to override that decision.[11] When Congress, pressured by the Grand Army of the Republic, passed a bill granting pensions for disabilities not caused by military service, Cleveland also vetoed that.[12] In his first term alone, Cleveland used the veto 414 times, which was more than four times more often than any previous president had used it.[13] In 1887, Cleveland issued his most well-known veto, that of the Texas Seed Bill.[14] After a drought had ruined crops in several Texas counties, Congress appropriated $100,000 (equivalent to $3,391,111 in 2023) to purchase seed grain for farmers there.[14] Cleveland vetoed the expenditure. In his veto message, he espoused a theory of limited government:
I can find no warrant for such an appropriation in the Constitution, and I do not believe that the power and duty of the general government ought to be extended to the relief of individual suffering which is in no manner properly related to the public service or benefit.
