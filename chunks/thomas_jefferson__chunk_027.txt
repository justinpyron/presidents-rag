Because of the tie, the election was decided by the Federalist-dominated House of Representatives.[18][b] Hamilton lobbied Federalist representatives on Jefferson's behalf, believing him a lesser political evil than Burr. On February 17, 1801, after thirty-six ballots, the House elected Jefferson president and Burr vice president.[19]
The win was marked by Democratic-Republican celebrations throughout the country.[20] Some of Jefferson's opponents argued that he owed his victory to the South's inflated number of electors.[21] Others alleged that Jefferson secured James Asheton Bayard's tie-breaking electoral vote by guaranteeing the retention of various Federalist posts in the government.[19] Jefferson disputed the allegation, and the historical record is inconclusive.[22]
The transition proceeded smoothly, marking a watershed in American history. As historian Gordon S. Wood writes, "it was one of the first popular elections in modern history that resulted in the peaceful transfer of power from one 'party' to another."[19]
