Pittsburgh exploded into riots next, but Hayes was reluctant to send in troops without the governor's request.[38] Other discontented citizens joined the railroad workers in rioting.[40] After a few days, Hayes resolved to send in troops to protect federal property wherever it appeared to be threatened and gave Major General Winfield Scott Hancock overall command of the situation, marking the first use of federal troops to break a strike against a private company.[38] The riots spread further, to Chicago and St. Louis, where strikers shut down railroad facilities.[38]
By July 29, the riots had ended and federal troops returned to their barracks.[41] No federal troops had killed any of the strikers, or been killed themselves, but clashes between state militia troops and strikers resulted in deaths on both sides.[42] The railroads were victorious in the short term, as the workers returned to their jobs and some wage cuts remained in effect.
