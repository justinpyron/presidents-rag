With pressure building for Eisenhower to demand Nixon's resignation from the ticket, Nixon went on television to address the nation on September 23, 1952.[6] The address, later named the Checkers speech, was heard by about 60 million Americans, which represented the largest audience ever for a television broadcast at that point.[7] In the speech, Nixon emotionally defended himself, stating that the fund was not secret and that his donors had not received special favors. He painted himself as a patriot and man of modest means, mentioning that his wife had no mink coat; instead, he said, she wore a "respectable Republican cloth coat".[6] The speech was remembered for the gift which Nixon had received, but which he would not give back, which he described as "a little cocker spaniel dog ...sent all the way from Texas. And our little girl—Tricia, the 6-year-old—named it Checkers."[6] The speech prompted a huge public outpouring of support for Nixon.[8] Eisenhower decided to retain him on the ticket,[9] and the ticket was victorious in the November election.[3]
Eisenhower granted Nixon more responsibilities during his term than any previous vice president.[10] Nixon attended Cabinet and National Security Council meetings and chaired them in Eisenhower's absence.
