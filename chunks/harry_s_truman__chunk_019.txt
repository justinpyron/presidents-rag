State and city party leaders strongly preferred Truman, and Roosevelt agreed.[3] Truman had repeatedly said that he was not in the race and that he did not want the vice presidency, and he remained reluctant.[3] One reason was that his wife and sister Mary Jane were both on his Senate staff payroll, and he feared negative publicity.[3] Truman did not campaign for the vice-presidential spot, though he welcomed the attention as evidence that he had become more than the "Senator from Pendergast".[4] Truman's nomination was dubbed the "Second Missouri Compromise" and was well received. The Roosevelt–Truman ticket achieved a 432–99 electoral-vote victory in the election, defeating the Republican ticket of Governor Thomas E. Dewey of New York and running mate Governor John Bricker of Ohio. Truman was sworn in as vice president on January 20, 1945.[5] After the inauguration, Truman called his mother, who instructed him, "Now you behave yourself."[6]
