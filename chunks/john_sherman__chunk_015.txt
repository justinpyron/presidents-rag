Reform of the nation's financial system continued in 1863 with the passage of the National Banking Act of 1863. This Act, first proposed by Chase in 1861 and introduced by Sherman two years later, established a series of nationally chartered private banks that would issue banknotes in coordination with the Treasury, replacing (though not completely) the system of state-chartered banks then in existence.[18] Although the immediate purpose was to fund the war, the National Bank Act was intended to be permanent, and remained the law until 1913.[19] A 10% tax on state banknotes passed in 1865 to encourage the shift to a national bank system.[18] Sherman agreed with Chase wholeheartedly and hoped that state banking would be eliminated.[20] Sherman believed the state-by-state system of regulation was disorderly and unable to facilitate the level of borrowing a modern nation might require.[21] He also believed the state banks were unconstitutional.[22] Not all Republicans shared Sherman's views, and when the Act eventually passed the Senate, it was by a narrow 23â€“21 vote.[20] Lincoln signed the bill into law on February 25, 1863.[23][d]
