After the election, Van Buren became suspicious that Clinton was working with the Federalist Party, and he broke from his former political ally.[7]
During the War of 1812, Van Buren worked with Clinton, Governor Tompkins, and Ambrose Spencer to support the Madison administration's prosecution of the war.[8] In addition, he was a special judge advocate appointed to serve as a prosecutor of William Hull during Hull's court-martial following the surrender of Detroit.[9][10] Anticipating another military campaign, he collaborated with Winfield Scott on ways to reorganize the New York Militia in the winter of 1814â€“1815, but the end of the war halted their work in early 1815.[11] Van Buren was so favorably impressed by Scott that he named his fourth son after him.[12] Van Buren's strong support for the war boosted his standing, and in 1815, he was elected to the position of New York Attorney General.
