Slavery
During Jackson's presidency, slavery remained a minor political issue.[128] Though federal troops were used to crush Nat Turner's slave rebellion in 1831,[129] Jackson ordered them withdrawn immediately afterwards despite the petition of local citizens for them to remain for protection.[130] Jackson considered the issue too divisive to the nation and to the delicate alliances of the Democratic Party.[131]
Jackson's view was challenged when the American Anti-Slavery Society agitated for abolition[132] by sending anti-slavery tracts through the postal system into the South in 1835.[131] Jackson condemned these agitators as "monsters"[133] who should atone with their lives[134] because they were attempting to destroy the Union by encouraging sectionalism.[135] The act provoked riots in Charleston, and pro-slavery Southerners demanded that the postal service ban distribution of the materials.
