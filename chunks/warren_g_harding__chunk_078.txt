 near the Oval Office, with Secret Service agents posted to ward off intruders.[9]
Despite being widely believed, Britton lost a libel lawsuit against a rebuttal of her book.[10]
Britton said that Harding had provided child support of $500 per month for the daughter he never met,[11] but that she had destroyed romantic correspondence from him at his request.[7][11]
Writing before Britton's claim was verified, Russell believed it unquestioningly,[6] while Dean, having reviewed Britton's papers at UCLA, regarded it as unproven.[12]
Sinclair suggested that a harsher standard was applied to Harding than to Grover Cleveland, who was elected president in 1884, although it was known he had a mistress and may have fathered a son out of wedlock.[10]
