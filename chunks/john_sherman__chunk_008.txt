House Leadership
The voters returned Sherman to office for a third term during 1858.[20] After a brief special session in March 1859, the 36th Congress adjourned, and Sherman and his wife went on vacation to Europe.[21] When they returned that December, the situation was similar to that of four years earlier: no party had an absolute majority. Republicans held 109 seats, Democrats 101, and the combination of Oppositionists and Know Nothings 27.[b][22][23] Again, sectional tension had increased while Congress was in recess, this time due to John Brown's raid on Harpers Ferry, Virginia.[23] The election for Speaker of the House promised to be contentious.[22] This time, Sherman was among the leading candidates, receiving the second-largest number of votes on the first ballot, with no candidate receiving a majority.[24] The election for Speaker was sidetracked immediately by a furor over an anti-slavery book, The Impending Crisis of the South, written by Hinton Rowan Helper and endorsed by many Republican members.[25] Southerners accused Sherman of having endorsed the book, but he protested that he had only endorsed its use as a campaign tool and had never read it.[26] After two months of balloting, no decision had been reached.[27] After their attempts to adopt a plurality rule failed, Sherm
