Main articles: Trump wall and 2018–2019 United States federal government shutdown
One of Trump's central campaign promises was to build a 1,000-mile (1,600 km) border wall to Mexico and have Mexico pay for it.[107] By the end of his term, the U.S. had built "40 miles [64 km] of new primary wall and 33 miles [53 km] of secondary wall" in locations where there had been no barriers and 365 miles (587 km) of primary or secondary border fencing replacing dilapidated or outdated barriers.[108]
In 2018, Trump refused to sign any appropriations bill from Congress unless it allocated $5.6 billion for the border wall,[109] resulting in the federal government partially shutting down for 35 days from December 2018 to January 2019, the longest U.S. government shutdown in history.[110][111] Around 800,000 government employees were furloughed or worked without pay.[112] Trump and Congress ended the shutdown by approving temporary funding that provided delayed payments to government workers, but no funds for the wall.[110] The shutdown resulted in an estimated permanent loss of $3 billion to the economy, according to the Congressional Budget Office.[113] About half of those polled blamed Trump for the shutdown, and Trump's approval ratings dropped.[114]
