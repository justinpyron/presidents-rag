Secretary of State
Cleveland recognized Bayard's status in the party hierarchy by offering him the top spot in his cabinet, Secretary of State.[1] Bayard did not think himself an expert in foreign affairs and enjoyed the sixteen years he had spent in the Senate; even so, he accepted the post and joined the administration.[2] Washington journalist Benjamin Perley Poore described Bayard's personality and habits at that time:Mr. Bayard has no appreciation of humor or fondness for political intrigue, and department drudgery would be intolerable to him were it not for his passionate fondness for out-door exercise. A bold horseman, an untiring pedestrian, and enthusiastic angler, and a good swimmer, he preserves his health, and gives close attention to the affairs of his Department.[3]
Samoa and Hawaii

Main article: Samoan crisis
Among the first foreign policy challenges Bayard and Cleveland faced was that concerning American influence in the Samoan Islands.[4] The United States, Great Britain, and Germany all had treaties with the Samoan government that guaranteed their right to trade and establish naval bases there.[5] In the 1880s, German chancellor Otto von Bismarck began to increase German influence in Samoa, and attempted to replace the Samoan king, Malietoa Laupepa, with Tamasese Titimaea, a claimant to the throne who favored German suzerainty.[5] Bayard and Cleveland opposed any change that would undermine Samoan independence, as did the British government.[6] Bayard
