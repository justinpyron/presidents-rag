Puzzled as to why no American university had undertaken the "continuous study of the causes, conduct and consequences of war",[28] Eisenhower undertook the creation of the Institute of War and Peace Studies, a research facility to "study war as a tragic social phenomenon".[29]  Eisenhower was able to use his network of wealthy friends and acquaintances to secure initial funding for it.[30] Under its founding director, international relations scholar William T. R. Fox, the institute began in 1951 and became a pioneer in international security studies, one that would be emulated by other institutes in the United States and Britain later in the decade.[28] The Institute of War and Peace Studies thus became one of the projects which Eisenhower considered his "unique contribution" to Columbia.[29] As the president of Columbia, Eisenhower gave voice to his opinions about the supremacy and difficulties of American democracy. His tenure marked his transformation from military to civilian leadership. His biographer Travis Beal Jacobs also suggested that the alienation of the Columbia faculty contributed to sharp intellectual criticism of him for many years.[31]
