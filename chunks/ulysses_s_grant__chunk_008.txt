Grant told Buchanan he would "resign if I don't reform."[49] On Sunday, Grant was found influenced by alcohol, but not incapacitated, at his company's paytable.[50] Keeping his pledge to Buchanan, Grant resigned, effective July 31, 1854.[51] Buchanan endorsed Grant's resignation but did not submit any report that verified the incident.[d][57] Grant did not face court-martial, and the War Department said: "Nothing stands against his good name."[58] Grant said years later, "the vice of intemperance (drunkenness) had not a little to do with my decision to resign."[59] With no means of support, Grant returned to St. Louis and reunited with his family.[60]
Civilian struggles, slavery, and politics

In 1854, at age 32, Grant entered civilian life, without any money-making vocation to support his growing family. It was the beginning of seven years of financial struggles and instability.[1] Grant's father offered him a place in the Galena, Illinois, branch of the family's leather business, but demanded Julia and the children stay in Missouri, with the Dents, or with the Grants in Kentucky.
