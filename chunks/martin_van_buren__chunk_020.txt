Seeking to ensure that Van Buren would replace Calhoun as his running mate, Jackson had arranged for a national convention of his supporters.[26] The May 1832 Democratic National Convention subsequently nominated Van Buren to serve as the party's vice presidential nominee.[27] Van Buren won the nomination over Philip P. Barbour (Calhoun's favored candidate) and Richard Mentor Johnson due to the support of Jackson and the strength of the Albany Regency.[28] Upon Van Buren's return from Europe in July 1832, he became involved in the Bank War, a struggle over the renewal of the charter of the Second Bank of the United States.[29]
Van Buren had long been distrustful of banks, and he viewed the bank as an extension of the Hamiltonian economic program, so he supported Jackson's veto of the bank's re-charter.[30] Henry Clay, the presidential nominee of the National Republicans, made the struggle over the bank the key issue of the presidential election of 1832.[31] The Jackson–Van Buren ticket won the 1832 election by a landslide,[32] and Van Buren took office as the eighth Vice President of the United States on March 4, 1833, at the age of 50.[33] During the Nullification Crisis, Van Buren counseled Jackson to pursue a policy of conciliation with South Carolina leaders.[34] He played little direct role in the passage of the Tariff of 1833, but he quietly hoped that the
