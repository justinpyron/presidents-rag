His longtime stance against the influence of corporations and the wealthy in American politics resonated with a dissatisfied citizenry troubled by the effects of income inequality in the U.S. and contrasted with Clinton's Wall Street ties.[8][7]
In the initial contest of the primaries season, Clinton only very narrowly won the Iowa Democratic caucuses, held February 1, over an increasingly popular Sanders[9][10] — the first woman to win them.[9] In the first primary, held in New Hampshire on February 9, she lost to Sanders by a wide margin.[11] Sanders was an increasing threat in the next contest, the Nevada caucuses on February 20,[12] but Clinton managed a five-percentage-point win, aided by final-days campaigning among casino workers.[13] Clinton followed that with a lopsided victory in the South Carolina primary on February 27.[12] These two victories stabilized her campaign and showed an avoidance of the management turmoil that harmed her 2008 effort.[12]
On March 1 Super Tuesday, Clinton won 7 of 11 contests, including a string of dominating victories across the South buoyed, as in South Carolina, by African-American voters. She opened up a significant lead in pledged delegates over Sanders.[14] She maintained this delegate lead across subsequent contests during the primary season, with a consistent pattern throughout.
