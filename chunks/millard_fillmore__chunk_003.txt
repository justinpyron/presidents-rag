Meanwhile, he became engaged to Abigail Powers.[23] In 1823 he was admitted to the bar, declined offers from Buffalo law firms, and returned to East Aurora to establish a practice as the town's only resident lawyer.[21][24] Later in life, Fillmore said he had initially lacked the self-confidence to practice in the larger city of Buffalo. His biographer, Paul Finkelman, suggested that after being under others' thumbs all his life, Fillmore enjoyed the independence of his East Aurora practice.[25] Millard and Abigail wed on February 5, 1826. They had two children, Millard Powers Fillmore and Mary Abigail Fillmore.[26]
Buffalo politician
Other members of the Fillmore family were active in politics and government in addition to Nathaniel's service as a justice of the peace.[a] Millard became interested in politics, and the rise of the Anti-Masonic Party in the late 1820s provided his entry.[3]

Many Anti-Masons were opposed to the presidential candidacy of General Andrew Jackson, who was a Mason.
