To buttress his hold on the party's nomination, Roosevelt made it clear that anyone opposing Cortelyou would be considered opposing the President.[69] The President secured his own nomination, but his preferred vice-presidential running mate, Robert R. Hitt, was not nominated.[70] Senator Charles Warren Fairbanks of Indiana, a favorite of conservatives, gained the nomination.[68]
While Roosevelt followed the tradition of incumbents in not actively campaigning on the stump, he sought to control the campaign's message through specific instructions to Cortelyou. He also attempted to manage the press's release of White House statements by forming the Ananias Club. Any journalist who repeated a statement made by the president without approval was penalized by restriction of further access.[71]
The Democratic Party's nominee in 1904 was Alton Brooks Parker.
