A military court ordered Louaillier's release, but Jackson kept him in prison and evicted Hall from the city.[51] Although Jackson lifted martial law when he received official word that the Treaty of Ghent, which ended the war with the British, had been signed,[52] his previous behavior tainted his reputation in New Orleans.[53]
Jackson's victory made him a national hero,[54] and on February 27, 1815, he was given the Thanks of Congress and awarded a Congressional Gold Medal.[55] Though the Treaty of Ghent had been signed in December 1814 before the Battle of New Orleans was fought,[56] Jackson's victory assured that the United States control of the region between Mobile and New Orleans would not be effectively contested by European powers. This control allowed the American government to ignore one of the articles in the treaty, which would have returned the Creek lands taken in the Treaty of Fort Jackson.[57]
