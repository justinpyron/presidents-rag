Lincoln's second state house campaign in 1834, this time as a Whig, was a success over a powerful Whig opponent.[1] Then followed his four terms in the Illinois House of Representatives for Sangamon County.[2] He championed construction of the Illinois and Michigan Canal, and later was a Canal Commissioner.[3] He voted to expand suffrage beyond white landowners to all white males, but adopted a "free soil" stance opposing both slavery and abolition.[4] In 1837, he declared, "[The] Institution of slavery is founded on both injustice and bad policy, but the promulgation of abolition doctrines tends rather to increase than abate its evils."[5] He echoed Henry Clay's support for the American Colonization Society which advocated a program of abolition in conjunction with settling freed slaves in Liberia.[6]
He was admitted to the Illinois bar on September 9, 1836,[7][8] and moved to Springfield and began to practice law under John T. Stuart, Mary Todd's cousin.[9] Lincoln emerged as a formidable trial combatant during cross-examinations and closing arguments. He partnered several years with Stephen T. Logan, and in 1844, began his practice with William Herndon, "a studious young man".[10]
