Slavery and Reconstruction
Besides his role in financial matters, Sherman also participated in debate over the conduct of the war and goals for the post-war nation. Sherman voted for the Confiscation Act of 1861, which allowed the government to confiscate any property being used to support the Confederate war effort (including slaves) and for the act abolishing slavery in the District of Columbia.[25] He also voted for the Confiscation Act of 1862, which clarified that slaves "confiscated" under the 1861 Act were freed.[26] In 1864, Sherman voted for the Thirteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution, abolishing slavery.[27] After some effort, it passed Congress and was ratified by the states the next year.[27]
When the session ended, Sherman campaigned in Indiana and Ohio for Lincoln's reelection.[28] In 1865, he attended Lincoln's second inauguration, then traveled to Savannah, Georgia to meet with his brother William, who had arrived there after his army's march to the sea.[29] Sherman returned home to Mansfield in April, where he learned of Lincoln's assassination[29] just days after the Confederate surrender.
