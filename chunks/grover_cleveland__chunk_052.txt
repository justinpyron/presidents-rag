Cleveland enjoyed many years of life after the tumor was removed, and there was some debate as to whether it was actually malignant. Several doctors, including Dr. Keen, stated after Cleveland's death that the tumor was a carcinoma.[66]
Other suggestions included ameloblastoma[67] or a benign salivary mixed tumor (also known as a pleomorphic adenoma).[68]
In the 1980s, analysis of the specimen finally confirmed the tumor to be verrucous carcinoma,[69] a low-grade epithelial cancer with a low potential for metastasis.[59]

Judicial appointments
Main article: List of federal judges appointed by Grover Cleveland
Cleveland's trouble with the Senate hindered the success of his nominations to the Supreme Court in his second term. In 1893, after the death of Samuel Blatchford, Cleveland nominated William B. Hornblower to the Court.[70] Hornblower, the head of a New York City law firm, was thought to be a qualified appointee, but his campaign against a New York machine politician had made Senator David B. Hill his enemy.[70] Further, Cleveland had not consulted the Senators before naming his appointee, leaving many who were already opposed to Cleveland on other grounds even more aggrieved.[70] The Senate rejected Hornblower's nomination on January 15, 1894, by a vote of 24 to 30.[70]
