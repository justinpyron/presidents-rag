In Kentucky and Indiana, Thomas worked as a farmer, cabinetmaker, and carpenter.[19] At various times he owned farms, livestock, and town lots, paid taxes, sat on juries, appraised estates, and served on county patrols. Thomas and Nancy were members of the Separate Baptist Church, "condemned profanity, intoxication, gossip, horse racing, and dancing." Most of its members opposed slavery.[20]
Overcoming financial challenges, Thomas in 1827 obtained clear title to 80 acres (32 ha) in Indiana, an area that became known as Little Pigeon Creek Community.[21]
Mother's death
On October 5, 1818, Nancy Lincoln died from milk sickness, leaving 11-year-old Sarah in charge of a household including her father, nine-year-old Abraham, and Nancy's 19-year-old orphan cousin, Dennis Hanks.[22] Ten years later, on January 20, 1828, Sarah died while giving birth to a stillborn son, devastating Lincoln.[23]
On December 2, 1819, Thomas married Sarah Bush Johnston, a widow from Elizabethtown, Kentucky, with three children of her own.[24] Abraham became close to his stepmother and called her "Mother".[25] Dennis Hanks said he was lazy, for all his "reading—scribbling—writing—ciphering—writing poetry".[26] His stepmother acknowledged he did not enjoy "physical labor" but loved to read.[27][28]
