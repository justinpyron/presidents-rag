His family summoned doctors James Craik, Gustavus Richard Brown, and Elisha C. Dick.[14] A fourth doctor, William Thornton, arrived some hours after Washington died.[15]
Brown initially believed Washington had quinsy; Dick thought the condition was a more serious "violent inflammation of the throat".[16] They continued the process of bloodletting to approximately five pints, but Washington's condition deteriorated further. Dick proposed a tracheotomy, but the other physicians were not familiar with that procedure and disapproved.[17] Washington instructed Brown and Dick to leave the room, while he assured Craik, "Doctor, I die hard, but I am not afraid to go."[18]
Washington's death came more swiftly than expected.[19] On his deathbed, out of fear of being entombed alive, he instructed his private secretary Tobias Lear to wait three days before his burial.[20] According to Lear, Washington died between 10 p.m. and 11 p.m. on December 14, 1799, with Martha seated at the foot of his bed. His last words were "'Tis well", from his conversation with Lear about his burial. He was 67.[21]
