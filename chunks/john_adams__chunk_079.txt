Religious views
According to biographer David McCullough, "Adams was both a devout Christian, and an independent thinker, and he saw no conflict in that."[16] He believed that regular church service was beneficial to man's moral sense.[17] Adams was raised in the Congregational church. In Quincy, the Unitarian faction was dominant and included Adams and his father. It was a new force in the colonies and denied the Trinity and the divinity of Jesus Christ. It was opposed by the Calvinist faction. In 1825, the Unitarians split off as a separate denomination that included John Adams.[18][19]
