Second term in the Senate
Mexican–American War and Wilmot Proviso
Calhoun was re-elected to the Senate in 1845 following the resignation of Daniel Elliott Huger. He soon became vocally opposed to the Mexican–American War. He believed that it would distort the national character by undermining republicanism in favor of empire and by bringing non-white persons into the country.[1] When Congress declared war against Mexico on May 13, he abstained from voting on the measure.[2] In South Carolina, Calhoun received some praise for his principled position, but support for the war was high in spite of his opposition.[3] Calhoun also vigorously opposed the Wilmot Proviso, an 1846 proposal by Pennsylvania Representative David Wilmot to ban slavery in all newly acquired territories.[4] The House of Representatives, through its Northern majority, passed the provision. However, the Senate never approved the measure.[4][5]
