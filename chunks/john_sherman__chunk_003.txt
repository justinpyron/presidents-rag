That Act, the brainchild of Illinois Democrat Stephen A. Douglas, opened the two named territories to slavery, an implicit repeal of the Missouri Compromise of 1820.[2] Intended to quiet national agitation over slavery by shifting the decision to local settlers, Douglas's Act instead inflamed anti-slavery sentiment in the North by allowing the possibility of slavery's expansion to territories held as free soil for three decades.[2] Two months after the Act's passage, Sherman became a candidate for Ohio's 13th congressional district.[3] A local convention nominated Sherman over two other candidates to represent what was then called the Opposition Party (later to become the Republican Party).
