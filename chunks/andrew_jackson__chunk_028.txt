Jackson enforced the Tenure of Office Act, an 1820 law that limited office tenure, authorized the president to remove current office holders, and appoint new ones.[10] During his first year in office, he removed about 10% of all federal employees[10] and replaced them with loyal Democrats.[11] Jackson argued that rotation in office reduced corruption[12] by making officeholders responsible to the popular will,[13] but it functioned as political patronage and became known as the spoils system.[14][12]
Petticoat affair
Main article: Petticoat affair
Jackson spent much of his time during his first two and a half years in office dealing with what came to be known as the "Petticoat affair" or "Eaton affair".[15][16] The affair focused on Secretary of War Eaton's wife, Margaret. She had a reputation for being promiscuous, and like Rachel Jackson, she was accused of adultery. She and Eaton had been close before her first husband John Timberlake died, and they married nine months after his death.[17] With the exception of Barry's wife Catherine,[18] the cabinet members' wives followed the lead of Vice-president Calhoun's wife Floride and refused to socialize with the Eatons.[19] Though Jackson defended Margaret, her presence split the cabinet, which had been so ineffective that he rarely called it into session,[2] and the ongoing disagreement led to its dissolution.[20]
