In mid-1899, the British inspector of Chinese maritime customs, Alfred Hippisley, visited the United States. In a letter to Rockhill, a friend, he urged that the United States and other powers agree to uniform Chinese tariffs, including in the enclaves. Rockhill passed the letter on to Hay,[13] and subsequently summarized the thinking of Hippisley and others, that there should be "an open market through China for our trade on terms of equality with all other foreigners".[14] Hay was in agreement, but feared Senate and popular opposition, and wanted to avoid Senate ratification of a treaty.[15] Rockhill drafted the first Open Door note, calling for equality of commercial opportunity for foreigners in China.[16]
