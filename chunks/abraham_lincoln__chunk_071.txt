Many others gathered along the tracks as the train passed with bands, bonfires, and hymn singing[17] or in silent grief. Poet Walt Whitman composed "When Lilacs Last in the Dooryard Bloom'd" to eulogize him, one of four poems he wrote about Lincoln.[18] African Americans were especially moved; they had lost their "Moses".[19] In a larger sense, the reaction was in response to the deaths of so many men in the war.[20] Historians emphasized the widespread shock and sorrow, but noted that some Lincoln haters celebrated his death.[21] Lincoln's body was buried at Oak Ridge Cemetery in Springfield and now lies within the Lincoln Tomb.[22]
Religious and philosophical beliefs
Further information: Religious views of Abraham Lincoln

As a young man Lincoln was a religious skeptic.[1] He was deeply familiar with the Bible, quoting and praising it.[2] He was private about his position on organized religion and respected the beliefs of others.[3] He never made a clear profession of Christian beliefs.[4] Throughout his public career, Lincoln often quoted Scripture.[5] His three most famous speeches—the House Divided Speech, the Gettysburg Address, and his second inaugural—all contain direct allusions to Providence and quote from Scripture.
