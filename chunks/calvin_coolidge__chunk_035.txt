Although not an isolationist, he was reluctant to enter into European involvements.[57] While Coolidge believed strongly in a non-interventionist foreign policy, he was a supporter of American exceptionalism.[58] Coolidge considered the 1920 Republican victory as a rejection of the Wilsonian position that the United States should join the League of Nations.[59] Coolidge believed the League did not serve American interests.[59] However, he spoke in favor of joining the Permanent Court of International Justice (World Court), provided that the nation would not be bound by advisory decisions.[60] In 1926, the Senate eventually approved joining the Court (with reservations).[61] The League of Nations accepted the reservations, but it suggested some modifications of its own.[62] The Senate failed to act and so the United States did not join the World Court.[62]
In 1924, the Coolidge administration nominated Charles Dawes to head the multi-national committee that produced the Dawes Plan.
