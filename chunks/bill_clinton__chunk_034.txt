The veteran gay rights and gay marriage activist Evan Wolfson has called these claims "historic revisionism".[51] Despite this, it has been noted that other than a brief written response to a Reader's Digest that questioned whether he agreed with it, Clinton had made no documented reference to the issue of gay marriage until May 1996.[53] In a July 2, 2011, editorial The New York Times opined, "The Defense of Marriage Act was enacted in 1996 as an election-year wedge issue, signed by President Bill Clinton in one of his worst policy moments."[54] Ultimately, in United States v. Windsor, the U.S. Supreme Court struck down DOMA in June 2013.[55]
