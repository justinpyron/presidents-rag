Though Nixon lost much popular support, even from his own party, he rejected accusations of wrongdoing and vowed to stay in office.[100] He admitted he had made mistakes but insisted he had no prior knowledge of the burglary, did not break any laws, and did not learn of the cover-up until early 1973.[102] On October 10, 1973, Vice President Agnew resigned for reasons unrelated to Watergate: he was convicted on charges of bribery, tax evasion and money laundering during his tenure as governor of Maryland. Believing his first choice, John Connally, would not be confirmed by Congress,[103] Nixon chose Gerald Ford, Minority Leader of the House of Representatives, to replace Agnew.[104] One researcher suggests Nixon effectively disengaged from his own administration after Ford was sworn in as vice president on December 6, 1973.[105]
On November 17, 1973, during a televised question-and-answer session[106] with 400 Associated Press managing editors, Nixon said, "People have got to know whether or not their president is a crook. Well, I'm not a crook. I've earned everything I've got."[107]
