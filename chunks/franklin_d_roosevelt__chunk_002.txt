Like most of his Groton classmates, Roosevelt went to Harvard College.[10] He was a member of the Alpha Delta Phi fraternity[13] and the Fly Club,[14] and served as a school cheerleader.[15] Roosevelt was relatively undistinguished as a student or athlete, but he became editor-in-chief of The Harvard Crimson daily newspaper, which required ambition, energy, and the ability to manage others.[16] He later said, "I took economics courses in college for four years, and everything I was taught was wrong."[17]
Roosevelt's father died in 1900, distressing him greatly.[18] The following year, Roosevelt's fifth cousin Theodore Roosevelt became U.S. president. Theodore's vigorous leadership style and reforming zeal made him Franklin's role model and hero.[19] He graduated from Harvard in three years in 1903 with an A.B. in history.[20] He remained there for a fourth year, taking graduate courses.[21] Like his cousin Theodore, he was a member of The Explorers Club.[22]
Roosevelt entered Columbia Law School in 1904, but dropped out in 1907 after passing the New York bar examination.[23][a] In 1908, he took a job with the prestigious law firm of Carter Ledyard & Milburn, working in the firm's admiralty law division.[25]
