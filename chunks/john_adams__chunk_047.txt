The Sedition Act made it a crime to publish "false, scandalous, and malicious writing" against the government or its officials.[23] Adams had not promoted any of these acts, but signed them in June 1798 at the urging of his wife and cabinet.[24]
The administration initiated fourteen or more indictments under the Sedition Act, as well as suits against five of the six most prominent Republican newspapers. The majority of the legal actions began in 1798 and 1799, and went to trial on the eve of the 1800 presidential election.[25] Vocal opponents of the Federalists were imprisoned or fined under the Sedition Act for criticizing the government.[26] Among them was Congressman Matthew Lyon of Vermont, who was sentenced to four months in jail for criticizing the President.[27] The alien acts were not stringently enforced because Adams resisted Secretary of State Timothy Pickering's attempts to deport aliens, although many left on their own, largely in response to the hostile environment.[25] Republicans were outraged. Jefferson, disgusted by the acts, wrote nothing publicly but partnered with Madison to secretly draft the Kentucky and Virginia Resolutions.
