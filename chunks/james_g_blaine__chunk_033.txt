Both candidates believed that the states of New York, New Jersey, Indiana, and Connecticut would determine the election.[15] In New York, Blaine received less support than he anticipated when Arthur and Conkling, still powerful in the New York Republican party, failed to actively campaign for him.[16] Blaine hoped that he would have more support from Irish Americans than Republicans typically did; while the Irish were mainly a Democratic constituency in the 19th century, Blaine's mother was Irish Catholic, and he believed his career-long opposition to the British government would resonate with the Irish.[17] Blaine's hope for Irish defections to the Republican standard were dashed late in the campaign when one of his supporters, Samuel D.
