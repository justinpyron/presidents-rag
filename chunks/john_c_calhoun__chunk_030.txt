From 1833 to 1834, Jackson was engaged in removing federal funds from the Second Bank of the United States during the Bank War. Calhoun opposed this action, considering it a dangerous expansion of executive power.[3] He called the men of the Jackson administration "artful, cunning, and corrupt politicians, and not fearless warriors".[4] He accused Jackson of being ignorant about financial matters. As evidence, he cited the economic panic caused by Nicholas Biddle as a means to stop Jackson from destroying the Bank.[4] On March 28, 1834, Calhoun voted with the Whig senators on a successful motion to censure Jackson for his removal of the funds.[5] In 1837, he refused to attend the inauguration of Jackson's chosen successor, Van Buren, even as other powerful senators who opposed the administration, such as Webster and Clay, did witness the inauguration.[6] However, by 1837, Calhoun generally had realigned himself with most of the Democrats' policies.[2]
