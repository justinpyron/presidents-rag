Soviet decline and thaw in relations
Further information: Cold War (1985–1991)
Although the Soviets did not accelerate military spending in response to Reagan's military buildup,[161] their enormous military expenses, in combination with collectivized agriculture and inefficient planned manufacturing, were a heavy burden for the Soviet economy. At the same time, the prices of oil, the primary source of Soviet export revenues, fell to one third of the previous level in 1985. These factors contributed to a stagnant economy during the tenure of Mikhail Gorbachev as Soviet leader.[162]
Reagan's foreign policy towards the Soviets wavered between brinkmanship and cooperation.[163] Reagan appreciated Gorbachev's revolutionary change in the direction of the Soviet policy and shifted to diplomacy, intending to encourage him to pursue substantial arms agreements.[97] They held four summit conferences between 1985 and 1988.[164] Reagan believed that if he could persuade the Soviets to allow for more democracy and free speech, this would lead to reform and the end of communism.[165] The critical summit was in Reykjavík in 1986, where they agreed to abolish all nuclear weapons.
