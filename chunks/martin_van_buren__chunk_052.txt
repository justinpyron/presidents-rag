Retirement
Van Buren never sought public office again after the 1848 election, but he continued to closely follow national politics. He was deeply troubled by the stirrings of secessionism in the South and welcomed the Compromise of 1850 as a necessary conciliatory measure despite his opposition to the Fugitive Slave Act of 1850.[37] Van Buren also worked on a history of American political parties and embarked on a tour of Europe, becoming the first former U.S. president to visit Britain.[38] Though still concerned about slavery, Van Buren and his followers returned to the Democratic fold, partly out of the fear that a continuing Democratic split would help the Whig Party.[39] He also attempted to reconcile the Barnburners and the Hunkers, with mixed results.[40]
Van Buren supported Franklin Pierce for president in 1852,[41] James Buchanan in 1856,[42] and Stephen A. Douglas in 1860.[43] Van Buren viewed the fledgling Know Nothing movement with contempt and felt that the anti-slavery Republican Party exacerbated sectional tensions.[44] He considered Chief Justice Roger Taney's ruling in the 1857 case of Dred Scott v.
