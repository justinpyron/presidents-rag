Reagan responded that bigotry was not in his nature[21] and later argued that certain provisions of the act infringed upon the rights of property owners.[22] After the Supreme Court of California ruled that the initiative that repealed the Rumford Act was unconstitutional in May, he voiced his support for the act's repeal,[23] but later preferred amending it.[24] In the Republican primary, Reagan defeated George Christopher,[25] a moderate Republican[26] who William F. Buckley Jr. thought had painted Reagan as extreme.[19]
Reagan's general election opponent, incumbent governor Pat Brown, attempted to label Reagan as an extremist and tout his own accomplishments.[27] Reagan portrayed himself as a political outsider,[28] and charged Brown as responsible for the Watts riots and lenient on crime.[27] In numerous speeches, Reagan "hit the Brown administration about high taxes, uncontrolled spending, the radicals at the University of California, Berkeley, and the need for accountability in government".[29] Meanwhile, many in the press perceived Reagan as "monumentally ignorant of state issues", though Lou Cannon said that Reagan benefited from an appearance he and Brown made on Meet the Press in September.[30] Ultimately, Reagan won the governorship with 57 percent of the vote compared to Brown's 42 percent.[31]
