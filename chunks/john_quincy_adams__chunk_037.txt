Jackson himself described the campaign as a "struggle between the virtue of the people and executive patronage".[57] Adams, meanwhile, refused to adapt to the new reality of political campaigns, and he avoided public functions and refused to invest in pro-administration tools such as newspapers.[58] In early 1827, Jackson was publicly accused of having encouraged his wife, Rachel, to desert her first husband.[59] In response, followers of Jackson attacked Adams's personal life, and the campaign turned increasingly nasty.[60] The Jacksonian press portrayed Adams as an out-of-touch elitist,[61] while pro-Adams newspapers attacked Jackson's past involvement in various duels and scuffles, portraying him as too emotional and impetuous for the presidency. Though Adams and Clay had hoped that the campaign would focus on the American System, it was instead dominated by the personalities of Jackson and Adams.[62]
