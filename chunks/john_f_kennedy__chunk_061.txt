Kennedy believed the grassroots movement for civil rights would anger many Southern Whites and make it more difficult to pass civil rights laws in Congress, and he distanced himself from it.[173] As articulated by Robert Kennedy, the administration's early priority was to "keep the president out of this civil rights mess."[174] Civil rights movement participants, mainly those on the front line in the South, viewed Kennedy as lukewarm,[172] especially concerning the Freedom Riders. In May 1961, the Congress of Racial Equality, led by James Farmer, organized integrated Freedom Rides to test a Supreme Court case ruling that declared segregation on interstate transportation illegal.[175] The Riders were repeatedly met with mob violence, including by federal and state law enforcement officers.[172] Kennedy assigned federal marshals to protect the Riders rather than using federal troops or uncooperative FBI agents.[172] Kennedy feared sending federal troops would stir up "hated memories of Reconstruction" among conservative Southern whites.[172] The Justice
