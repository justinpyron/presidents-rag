Cleveland's service as sheriff was unremarkable. Biographer Rexford Tugwell described the time in office as a waste for Cleveland politically. Cleveland was aware of graft in the sheriff's office during his tenure and chose not to confront it.[7] A notable incident of his term took place on September 6, 1872, when Patrick Morrissey was executed. He had been convicted of murdering his mother.[8] As sheriff, Cleveland was responsible for either personally carrying out the execution or paying a deputy $10 to perform the task.[8] In spite of reservations about the hanging, Cleveland executed Morrissey himself.[8] He hanged another murderer, John Gaffney, on February 14, 1873.[9]
After his term as sheriff ended, Cleveland returned to his law practice, opening a firm with his friends Lyman K. Bass and Wilson S. Bissell.[10] Bass was later replaced by George J. Sicard.[11]  Elected to Congress in 1872, Bass did not spend much time at the firm, but Cleveland and Bissell soon rose to the top of Buffalo's legal community.[12] Up to that point, Cleveland's political career had been honorable and unexceptional.
