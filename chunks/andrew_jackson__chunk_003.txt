War, Jackson worked as a saddler,[1] briefly returned to school, and taught reading and writing to children.[2] In 1784, he left the Waxhaws region for Salisbury, North Carolina, where he studied law under attorney Spruce Macay.[3] He completed his training under John Stokes,[4] and was admitted to the North Carolina bar in September 1787.[5] Shortly thereafter, his friend John McNairy helped him get appointed as a prosecuting attorney in the Western District of North Carolina,[6] which would later become the state of Tennessee. While traveling to assume his new position, Jackson stopped in Jonesborough. While there, he bought his first slave, a woman who was around his age.[7] He also fought his first duel, accusing another lawyer, Waightstill Avery, of impugning his character. The duel ended with both men firing in the air.[8]
Jackson began his new career in the frontier town of Nashville in 1788 and quickly moved up in social status.[9] He became a protégé of William Blount, one of the most powerful men in the territory.[10] Jackson was appointed attorney general of the Mero District in 1791 and judge-advocate for the militia the following year.[11] He also got involved in land speculation,[12] eventually forming a partnership with fellow lawyer John Overton.[13] Their partnership mainly dealt with claims made under a "land grab" act of 1783 that opened Cherokee and Chickasaw territory to North Carolina's white residents.[14] Jackson also became a slave trader,[15] transpo
