Cleveland brought his opposition to needless spending to the governor's office. He promptly sent the legislature eight vetoes in his first two months in office.[38] The first to attract attention was his veto of a bill to reduce the fares on New York City elevated trains to five cents.[39] The bill had broad support because the trains' owner, Jay Gould, was unpopular, and his fare increases were widely denounced.[40] Cleveland saw the bill as unjustâ€”Gould had taken over the railroads when they were failing and had made the system solvent again.[41] Cleveland believed that altering Gould's franchise would violate the Contract Clause of the federal Constitution.[41] Despite the initial popularity of the fare-reduction bill, the newspapers praised Cleveland's veto.[41] Theodore Roosevelt, then a member of the Assembly, had reluctantly voted for the bill with the intention of holding railroad barons accountable.[42] After the veto, Roosevelt and other legislators reversed their position, and Cleveland's veto was sustained.[42]
