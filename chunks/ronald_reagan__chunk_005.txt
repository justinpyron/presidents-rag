Throughout his military service, Reagan produced over 400 training films.[22]
Screen Actors Guild presidency
When Robert Montgomery resigned as president of the Screen Actors Guild (SAG) on March 10, 1947, Reagan was elected to that position in a special election.[24] Reagan's first tenure saw various labor–management disputes,[25] the Hollywood blacklist,[26] and the Taft–Hartley Act's implementation.[27] On April 10, the Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI) interviewed Reagan and he provided them with the names of actors whom he believed to be communist sympathizers.[28] During a House Un-American Activities Committee hearing, Reagan testified that some guild members were associated with the Communist Party[29] and that he was well-informed about a "jurisdictional strike".[30] When asked if he was aware of communist efforts within the Screen Writers Guild, he called information about the efforts "hearsay".[31] Reagan resigned as SAG president November 10, 1952, but remained on the boar
