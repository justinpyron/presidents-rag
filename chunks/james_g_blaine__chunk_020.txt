On monetary issues, Blaine continued the advocacy for a strong dollar that he had begun as a Representative.[6] This stance was in opposition to Senate Republican leadership, including Senate President Pro Tempore Thomas W. Ferry, who generally supported the greenback movement.[7] The issue had shifted from debate over greenbacks to debate over which metal should back the dollar: gold and silver, or gold alone.[6] The Coinage Act of 1873 stopped the coinage of silver for all coins worth a dollar or more, effectively tying the dollar to the value of gold. As a result, the money supply contracted and the effects of the Panic of 1873 grew worse, making it more expensive for debtors to pay debts they had entered into when currency was less valuable.[8] Farmers and laborers, especially, clamored for the return of coinage in both metals, believing the increased money supply would restore wages and property values.[9] Democratic Representative Richard P.
