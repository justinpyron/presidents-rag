In 1999, with the merger of Exxon and Mobil, he was named executive vice president of ExxonMobil Development Company. In 2004, he became president and director of ExxonMobil.[4] Upon this appointment Tillerson's replacement of Lee Raymond as CEO of Exxon Mobil was implied.[5] His major competitor was Ed Galante, another Exxon executive.[6] On January 1, 2006, Tillerson was elected chairman and CEO, following the retirement of Lee Raymond.[1] At the time, ExxonMobil had 80,000 employees, did business in nearly 200 countries, and had an annual revenue of nearly $400 billion.[2]
Under Tillerson's leadership, ExxonMobil cooperated closely with Saudi Arabia, the world's largest oil exporter and a longtime U.S. ally, as well as Qatar and the United Arab Emirates.[7] From 2003 to 2005, a European subsidiary of ExxonMobil, Infineum, operated in the Middle East providing sales to Iran, Sudan and Syria. ExxonMobil leaders said they followed all legal frameworks, and that such sales were minuscule compared to their annual revenue of $371 billion at the time.[8] In 2009, ExxonMobil acquired XTO Energy, a major natural gas producer, for $31 billion in stock.
