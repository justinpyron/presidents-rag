Jackson had Federal funds deposited into state banks friendly to the administration's policies, which critics called pet banks.[107] The number of these state banks more than doubled during Jackson's administration,[100] and investment patterns changed. The Bank, which had been the federal government's fiscal agent, invested heavily in trade and financed interregional and international trade. State banks were more responsive to state governments and invested heavily in land development, land speculation, and state public works projects.[108] In spite of the efforts of Taney's successor, Levi Woodbury, to control them, the pet banks expanded their loans, helping to create a speculative boom in the final years of Jackson's administration.[109]
